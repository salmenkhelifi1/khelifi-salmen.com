## A Year of Open Books: What I Learned Sharing My SaaS Revenue Publicly

A year ago, I did something that felt counter-intuitive for a solo founder: I started sharing my SaaS revenue publicly. This wasn't just about transparency; it was an experiment that fundamentally reshaped my product, my business strategy, and my connection with the indie hacker community.

I'd seen others do it, but always from a distance. The idea of exposing my numbers, especially when they were small, felt vulnerable. But the promise of accountability and community engagement was too strong to ignore. I wanted to see if 'building in public' could truly accelerate growth. The results were far more impactful than I anticipated.



## The Leap of Faith: Setting Up the Transparency Engine

**What I did:** My first step was to choose a platform. I considered a custom dashboard, but opted for a simpler, manual approach initially. Each month, I'd update a dedicated public page on my product's website with key metrics: MRR, active users, churn rate, and new sign-ups. I linked to this page from my Twitter bio and mentioned it in my monthly updates.

**What I learned:**
*   The initial setup was surprisingly easy, but the mental hurdle was immense. The fear of judgment, of looking 'unsuccessful,' was real. I learned that **perfection is the enemy of progress** here. Just getting the first numbers out, no matter how small, was the biggest win.
*   The immediate feedback was overwhelmingly positive. It wasn't about the size of the numbers, but the act of sharing. People appreciated the honesty. This taught me that the indie hacker community values transparency more than immediate success metrics.

## The Accountability Loop: Data-Driven Discipline

**What I did:** Every month, around the 5th, I'd dedicate an hour to compiling the previous month's data. This wasn't just about pulling numbers; it involved a mini-retrospective. I'd look at what changed, what campaigns ran, and how they impacted the metrics. Then, I'd write a short summary post explaining the 'why' behind the numbers. For instance, calculating churn wasn't just a number; it was `(Customers Lost / Customers at Start of Period) * 100`. Understanding each component was key.

```python
# Example: Simple MRR calculation logic
def calculate_mrr(subscriptions):
    total_mrr = 0
    for sub in subscriptions:
        if sub.is_active and not sub.is_trial:
            total_mrr += sub.monthly_price
    return total_mrr

# This kind of internal logic became clearer when explaining public numbers.
```

**What I learned:**
*   This forced monthly review became an **invaluable accountability mechanism**. Before, I might let a bad month slide without deep analysis. Now, knowing I had to explain it publicly, I dug deeper. It pushed me to understand my metrics intimately, not just superficially.
*   It highlighted trends I might have missed. For example, a consistent dip in sign-ups on weekends became apparent, leading me to adjust my content publishing schedule. Or, a sudden spike after a specific feature launch clearly demonstrated its impact. This data-driven approach, fueled by public commitment, was a game-changer for **identifying actionable insights**.



## Deepening Audience Connection & Product Strategy

**What I did:** Beyond just sharing numbers, I actively encouraged questions and discussions around them. I'd ask: 'What do these numbers tell you?' or 'What would you focus on if these were your metrics?' This wasn't just passive sharing; it was an invitation to collaborate.

**What I learned:**
*   The community became an extended advisory board. Users and fellow founders offered insights I hadn't considered. For instance, after a public discussion about churn, several users pointed out a specific onboarding friction point I had overlooked. This led to a **direct product improvement** that reduced churn by 5% the following month.
*   It built immense trust. When users saw the raw numbers, they understood the journey. This transparency fostered a sense of partnership. They weren't just customers; they were part of the story. This translated into more candid feedback, higher engagement in beta programs, and even **organic evangelism** for the product.
*   Publicly discussing pricing changes or new feature ideas with the revenue data as context made those conversations much more productive. It wasn't abstract; it was grounded in the financial reality of the business. This helped validate pricing tiers and prioritize features based on perceived value and potential impact on growth.

## The Trade-offs and Unexpected Challenges

**What I did:** While the benefits were significant, it wasn't always smooth sailing. I encountered skepticism, direct competition leveraging my public data, and the occasional negative comment.

**What I learned:**
*   **Managing expectations** was crucial. Not every month is a hockey stick. There were flat months, even down months. Publicly acknowledging these, explaining the reasons, and outlining the plan to address them was vital. It reinforced authenticity rather than undermining it.
*   Competition is real. Sharing numbers means competitors know your growth rate, your pricing strategy, and potentially your weaknesses. I learned to focus on my unique value proposition and execution, rather than getting distracted by what others might do with the information. The benefits of community and accountability outweighed the competitive risk for my specific niche.
*   The mental load can be higher. There's a constant awareness that your business performance is under public scrutiny. Developing a thick skin and a clear boundary between public sharing and private strategy became essential. Not every detail needs to be public, but the core metrics should be.



## The Path Forward: Continuous Transparency

A year of public revenue sharing has been transformative. It wasn't just a marketing tactic; it was a core operational shift. It provided unparalleled accountability, cultivated a deeply engaged community, and directly informed critical product and business decisions.

For solo founders and indie hackers, I highly recommend considering this path. Start small. Share one key metric. See how your audience reacts. The benefits of building in public, especially with financial transparency, can far outweigh the initial discomfort.

I plan to continue this practice, perhaps evolving the metrics I share as the business matures. The next step is to integrate more qualitative feedback directly into the public updates, showing how community input directly translates into product changes. This continuous feedback loop is the engine of sustainable growth.
